01 · The Market Shifted
Four things broke open at the same time.
The World Cup opened the door.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup is the first men's tournament in history to mandate concussion spotters at every match and allow dedicated concussion substitutions. The question "what actually helps after a concussion?" is being asked by millions of people who never asked it before. That question is a door. And what is on the other side of it is not a sports market — it is an epidemic.

Sports is the hook. It is not the market.

In the UK, sport accounts for approximately 10% of traumatic brain injuries. Road traffic accidents account for 50%. Falls account for 21%. Physical assault — including domestic violence — accounts for 12%. (Source: Bolt Burdon Kemp / Headway UK) Concussion is not primarily a sports problem. It is a public health crisis that sport has just made visible.

The domestic violence data is the most urgent signal — and the most important to hold carefully.

In England and Wales, 1 in 2 people who have experienced domestic abuse may be living with a brain injury. Up to 92% of blows in intimate partner violence are directed to the head, face, or neck. 3.8 million people experienced domestic abuse in 2024/25. (Source: ONS Crime Survey, 2025) This is not a niche. This is a population that is injured, underdiagnosed, and almost entirely without specialist support.

The research landed — and it points directly at CST.

The Ricky Williams Concussion Pilot Program produced statistically significant results across every measured dimension: memory (P=0.0156), visual-motor reaction time (P=0.0332), pain reduction (P=0.0448), cervical range of motion (P=0.0377). Sleep duration doubled over five days with continued improvement at the 3-month follow-up. (Published: Medical Acupuncture, November 2017) No subsequent research has contradicted these findings.
